This is a practical immersion-watchability catalog: **278 actual YouTube
channels**, with 104 for English, 61 for Spanish, 55 for German, and 58 for
French. It includes structured teachers, comprehensible-input creators, graded
listening libraries, pronunciation specialists, and native-interest channels
that provide a useful next step beyond learner material.

**Snapshot:** July 13, 2026. Channel names, handles, video libraries, caption
tracks, and publishing schedules can change. Every row links the exact channel
checked for this catalog; caption notes describe the useful pattern, not a
promise that every upload has the same tracks.

## Legend

- **Level:** CEFR bands describe the useful range of the channel, not a claim
  that every video fits that level. A wide range such as A1–C1 usually means the
  channel organizes distinct beginner, intermediate, and advanced material.
- **Channel character:** Structured course, explicit teaching, comprehensible
  input, graded stories, interviews, news, culture, pronunciation, exam
  preparation, or native-interest content.
- **Caption and transcript support:** **Creator captions** are uploaded or
  reviewed by the creator. **Auto-captions** are generated by YouTube and can
  mishear names, dialect, reductions, music, and specialist vocabulary.
- **Listening profile:** The pace, clarity, accent range, overlap, production
  style, and vocabulary that shape real listening difficulty.
- **Best use:** The specific study activity the channel supports well—not a
  generic instruction to “watch more videos.”
- **Evidence:** **Channel** identifies the exact source; **Official level** or
  **Official course** means the creator publishes that structure; **Human**
  means a learner or teacher report; **Editorial** means this list's level and
  practical-use judgment.

Purpose-built A1–A2 channels earn a beginner band through slow or graded
speech, visual scaffolding, repetition, and controlled vocabulary. Short
videos, animation, or a friendly presenter do not automatically make a channel
beginner-friendly. Native-interest channels generally begin at B1 or B2 even
when their narration is exceptionally clear.

## Caption and availability traps

- A channel can mix creator captions, auto-captions, and videos with no caption
  track. Check the exact video before building a study routine around it.
- YouTube's transcript is generated from the available caption track. A visible
  transcript does not prove that a human reviewed the wording.
- Translated subtitles practice reading in the translated language; they do not
  show whether you understood the target-language audio.
- Auto-captions are least dependable with overlapping speakers, strong regional
  speech, code-switching, songs, street audio, and specialist terminology.
- Shorts are convenient review material but often use faster cuts, denser text,
  and less context than a channel's long-form videos.
- Old or dormant channels can still contain excellent evergreen libraries. The
  catalog does not claim a current upload schedule unless the evidence says so.

## Evidence policy

Every row links the exact YouTube channel. The level, listening profile, caption
strategy, and best use remain **Editorial** unless a separate official course
or human source is linked. No subscriber counts, popularity ranks, or copied
proprietary difficulty scores are used.

Teaching channels and native-interest channels solve different problems. The
former explain or control the language; the latter provide authentic subject
matter after a learner can tolerate more lexical density. The table identifies
the distinction instead of putting both under one generic difficulty score.

## Contributing

A useful contribution should include:

1. the exact channel URL and displayed name;
2. the target language, regional variety, and teaching language;
3. two or more representative videos from the recommended level;
4. whether their captions are creator-provided, auto-generated, or absent;
5. concrete observations about pace, accents, overlap, and vocabulary; and
6. the videos and date checked.

Keep each table alphabetized by displayed channel name. Separate official
course levels, direct observations, and editorial judgments. Do not infer a
level from video length, thumbnails, animation, or subscriber count.
